Islam

Introduction

Islam is one of the world’s major monotheistic religions. It was founded in the 7th century by the Prophet Muhammad in Arabia. The word Islam means “surrender”, showing that Muslims aim to submit to the will of Allah (God in Arabic). Muslims believe that Muhammad is the last prophet after others like Abraham, Moses, and Jesus, and they follow the Qur’an, believed to be the final word of God.

Today, 1.6–1.9 billion Muslims live across the globe, mostly in the Asia-Pacific, Middle East, and Africa. While cultures vary, Muslims share core beliefs and a sense of belonging to a global religious community (the ummah).

Core Beliefs

Oneness of God (Tawḥīd): Islam teaches belief in one, all-powerful God (Allah). Worship is only for Allah, and associating anything else with Him is seen as a major sin.

Prophets and Revelation: Muslims believe Allah sent prophets to guide humanity, and Muhammad was the last. The Qur’an is the final and complete revelation, while earlier texts like the Torah and Gospel are respected but not seen as fully preserved.

Angels, Judgment, and Divine Decree: Muslims believe in angels and in a Day of Judgment, when everyone will be held accountable for their actions. Those judged righteously will enter Paradise, others will face punishment. God’s will (qadar) is powerful, but human choice still matters.

Historical Background

Islam began in 610 CE when Muhammad received his first revelations in Mecca. After facing resistance, he and his followers moved to Medina in 622 CE (an event called the Hijra), marking the start of the Islamic calendar. Islam quickly expanded across Arabia and beyond.

After Muhammad’s death, leadership passed to his companions (the Rashidun Caliphs), and within a century, Islam spread from Spain to India. Great Islamic empires like the Umayyads, Abbasids, and Ottomans helped grow Islamic civilization in areas like science, medicine, and architecture.

A major event was the Sunni–Shia split, which started over disagreements about leadership. This created two main branches that remain today.


Branches

Sunni Islam: The largest branch (about 85–90%), Sunnis believe in elected leadership and follow four main legal schools. They emphasize tradition and community consensus.

Shia Islam: About 10–15% of Muslims. Shias believe leadership should have stayed within Muhammad’s family, starting with Ali. The largest Shia group is the Twelvers, found in Iran, Iraq, and Lebanon

Other Groups and Traditions: Smaller communities include the Ibadis (mainly in Oman), the Ahmadiyya (not recognized by most Sunni and Shia groups), and Sufis, who focus on spiritual practices and inner closeness to God

Practices and Worships

Five Pillars: The Five Pillars of Islam are the foundation of a Muslim's faith and daily life. The first pillar is the Shahada, or declaration of faith, which states: “There is no god but God, and Muhammad is the Messenger of God.” This simple yet powerful statement affirms monotheism and marks a person’s entry into Islam. The second pillar is Salah, the practice of praying five times a day while facing the holy city of Mecca. These prayers—performed at dawn, noon, afternoon, sunset, and evening—create a rhythm of devotion and reflection throughout the day. Muslims may pray individually or in groups, and every Friday at noon, a special prayer and sermon is held at the mosque.

The third pillar is Zakat, or almsgiving. Muslims are required to donate a small portion of their wealth each year (usually 2.5%) to help those in need. This act of charity purifies wealth and strengthens social responsibility and care for the poor. The fourth pillar is Sawm, or fasting during the month of Ramadan. From dawn to sunset, Muslims avoid food, drink, and other physical needs. Fasting encourages discipline, spiritual growth, and empathy for those who experience hunger daily. Finally, the fifth pillar is the Hajj, the pilgrimage to Mecca, which every Muslim must attempt at least once in their lifetime if they are physically and financially able. During Hajj, millions gather to perform sacred rituals that honor the devotion of Abraham and reaffirm their submission to God.

Society

Islam is both a personal belief and a way of life. Sharia, or Islamic law, comes from the Qur’an and Muhammad’s teachings and guides areas like family, business, and ethics. Its practice varies by country and culture.

Islamic values include justice, charity, mercy, and responsibility. Muslims are expected to care for the needy and treat others fairly. Institutions like charity (zakat) and religious endowments (waqf) have helped build schools, hospitals, and mosques. In history, Islamic societies often allowed Jews and Christians to live peacefully under protection. Today, Islam continues to influence clothing, family life, education, and the arts. While beliefs are shared, cultural practices can differ widely across Muslim communities around the world.
