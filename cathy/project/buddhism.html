Buddhism

Introduction

Buddhism is a major world religion that began in northern India around the 5th century BCE. It was founded by Siddhartha Gautama, who became known as the Buddha, or “the Enlightened One.” The main goal of Buddhism is to end human suffering (called dukkha) through right living, meditation, and understanding. From India, Buddhism spread across Asia—including China, Japan, Korea, Southeast Asia, and Tibet—and influenced many cultures. Today, there are about 488 million Buddhists worldwide, making up around 7% of the global population. The three main branches are Theravada, Mahayana, and Vajrayana Buddhism.

Core Beliefs

  • Four Noble Truths: Fundamental insights that suffering exists, has causes, can cease, and there is a path out of it
  • Eightfold Path: The practical guide to right understanding, ethics and meditation leading to enlightenment (right view, intention, speech, etc.)
  • Karma and Rebirth: Moral actions have consequences across lifetimes; liberation (nirvana) ends the cycle of rebirth
  • No-Self (Anatman): Buddhism teaches there is no permanent ego or soul, a realization that underlies its meditation practices
  • Three Jewels: Refuge in the Buddha, the Dharma (teachings), and the Sangha (monastic community) provides guidance and support on the path

Historical Background

Buddhism began with the life and teachings of Siddhartha Gautama in India around 480–400 BCE. After his death, followers preserved his teachings in councils. In the 3rd century BCE, Emperor Ashoka of India helped spread Buddhism across Asia by supporting it in his empire and sending out missionaries. Over time, different forms of Buddhism developed.


Branches

Theravada Buddhism (Teaching of the Elders) focuses on individual enlightenment and becoming an arhat, a person who is freed from rebirth. It follows the earliest scriptures written in the Pali language. It is practiced mainly in Southeast Asia.

Mahayana Buddhism (Great Vehicle) teaches the Bodhisattva path—seeking enlightenment to help all beings. It includes many schools like Zen, Pure Land, and Nichiren. It spread across East Asia.

Vajrayana Buddhism (Tantric or Esoteric Buddhism) developed later and uses special rituals, mantras, and meditation methods. It is practiced in Tibet, Bhutan, Mongolia, and parts of India and Nepal. Despite differences, all three branches believe in the Four Noble Truths, karma, and reaching nirvana.

Practices and Worships

Buddhists express their beliefs through meditation, ethical living, and acts of devotion.

  • Mediation and chanting are important in all schools. Theravada monks use insight meditation, Zen Buddhists practice silent sitting, and Vajrayana Buddhists use visualizations and mantras
  • Worship includes making offerings at temples or in front of Buddha statues (flowers, candles, incense), and celebrating holy days like Vesak, which marks the Buddha’s birth, enlightenment, and death.
  • Monks and nuns live in monasteries, follow strict rules, and teach the community
  • Lay People support the monastics with food and donations and follow the Five Precepts (not killing, stealing, lying, misusing sex, or taking intoxicants)

Society

Buddhist values such as compassion, non-violence, and generosity have shaped many cultures. Leaders like Emperor Ashoka used Buddhist ideas to promote kindness and truth in government. Buddhist monasteries often became centers for education, health care, and social help.

In modern times, Buddhist ideas influence society in many ways. In Asia, festivals and holidays often have Buddhist roots. Around the world, mindfulness and meditation practices from Buddhism are used for mental health and stress relief. Some Buddhists also practice Engaged Buddhism, which applies Buddhist teachings to issues like peace, justice, and the environment. Buddhism continues to promote kindness, awareness, and ethical living wherever it is followed.
