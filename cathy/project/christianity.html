Christianity

Introduction

Christianity is one of the world’s largest religions, based on the life and teachings of Jesus of Nazareth. Christians believe in one God expressed as the Trinity—the Father, the Son (Jesus), and the Holy Spirit. They view Jesus as the Messiah who was crucified and rose from the dead to bring salvation to humanity.

The religion began in the 1st century in Palestine and quickly spread across the Roman Empire. Today, Christianity has about 2.2 billion followers worldwide, making up roughly one-third of the global population. It is divided into three major branches: Roman Catholicism, Eastern Orthodoxy, and Protestantism, with many smaller denominations and traditions within each group.

Core Beliefs

While Christian practices differ across traditions, all Christians share key beliefs. They regard the Bible (both Old and New Testaments) as sacred scripture and affirm belief in one God, Jesus Christ as God’s Son, and the Holy Spirit. Christians believe that sin separates humanity from God, but salvation is possible through Jesus’ death and resurrection, which is seen as a gift of grace received by faith. Most Christians also believe in a future resurrection and eternal life, as stated in ancient Christian creeds. Morally, Christians are taught to love God and others, forgive, and work toward justice and compassion.

Historical Background

Christianity began in the early 1st century C.E. within a Jewish context, founded by Jesus of Nazareth. After his death, his followers spread his teachings across the Roman Empire. Though Christians were originally persecuted, the religion gained legal status under Emperor Constantine in the 4th century and became a major force in Europe. In 1054 C.E., tensions led to the East–West Schism, dividing the Eastern Orthodox and Roman Catholic churches. Later, the Protestant Reformation in the 16th century, led by figures like Martin Luther, resulted in new Christian denominations. Through colonialism and missionary work, Christianity eventually spread worldwide and is now practiced on every continent.


Branches

Christianity is divided into several major traditions. The largest branch is Roman Catholicism, led by the Pope in Rome. It is organized worldwide and emphasizes the seven sacraments (like baptism and the Eucharist), with the Church and its clergy seen as having authority in teaching and interpreting faith. Eastern Orthodoxy is made up of self-governing churches (like Greek, Russian, and Serbian Orthodox) that share similar beliefs and rituals, including the sacraments, but do not accept the Pope’s authority. Protestantism includes many denominations such as Lutheran, Anglican, Presbyterian, Baptist, Methodist, and Evangelical churches. It developed during the Reformation and focuses on the Bible’s authority, salvation through faith, and the idea that all believers can connect with God directly. There are also smaller Christian traditions, like the Oriental Orthodox churches, the Church of the East, and Restorationist groups, but they all share belief in Jesus Christ and the New Testament.

Practices and Worships

Christianity involves both public worship and private devotion. Most Christians gather on Sundays for services that include prayers, singing, Scripture readings, and preaching. A central ritual is the Eucharist (also called Communion or the Lord’s Supper), which remembers Jesus’ last meal with his disciples. Baptism is practiced across all Christian traditions as a sign of entry into the faith.

Catholic and Orthodox churches observe seven sacraments (like confirmation, marriage, and anointing of the sick), while most Protestant churches recognize only two—baptism and Communion. Christians also engage in personal prayer and Bible reading. They worship follows a liturgical calendar, with major events like Christmas (celebrating Jesus’ birth) and Easter (honoring his resurrection). Easter is the most important Christian holiday and is preceded by Lent and Holy Week. Other observances include Pentecost and saints’ days. Worship styles differ across churches—some are formal and ritualistic, others are simple or modern—but all emphasize community, prayer, and moral values.

Society

Christianity has greatly shaped society and culture. Historically, Christian institutions helped establish many of Europe’s first universities and hospitals. Christians have led global efforts in charity, education, and healthcare. Christian art, music, and literature have influenced Western civilization, and holidays like Christmas and Easter are now celebrated even in many secular societies.

Christianity is now a global and diverse religion. It has grown from 600 million in 1910 to over 2 billion today, mainly due to high birth rates and conversions in Africa, Asia, and Latin America. At the same time, church attendance and Christian identity have declined in parts of Europe and North America.
