Judaism

Introduction

Judaism is one of the oldest religions in the world and is practiced by people known as Jews. It teaches belief in one God who made a special agreement, called a covenant, with early leaders like Abraham and Moses. Jewish teachings are found in the Hebrew Bible, especially the Torah (the first five books), and also in later writings by rabbis.

Today, about 14 to 16 million people follow Judaism. It is more than just a religion—it is also a culture and a way of life. Jewish traditions include holidays, food, family customs, and values. Some Jews follow all religious rules, while others focus more on cultural practices, but many feel proud to be Jewish and feel connected to the Jewish community.

Core Beliefs

Judaism is based on monotheism, the belief in one God who is just, caring, and expects people to live morally. The most important prayer is the Shema, which says: “Hear, O Israel: the Lord is our God, the Lord is One.” Jews believe that God made a covenant with Abraham and later gave the Torah (God’s law) to Moses on Mount Sinai. Because of this covenant, Jews are expected to follow God’s laws and live in a way that sets a good example for others.

Jewish law is called Halakha and comes from both the written Torah and the Oral Torah (rabbinic writings like the Talmud). There are 613 commandments (called mitzvot) that include rules for prayer, holidays, food, business, family, and treating others fairly.

Historical Background

Judaism began over 3,000 years ago in the Middle East. According to tradition, the Jewish people came from Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. They were enslaved in Egypt but were led out by Moses, who received the Torah from God. The Israelites later built the First Temple in Jerusalem under King David and Solomon.

In 586 BCE, the Babylonians destroyed the First Temple and many Jews were exiled. They returned later and built the Second Temple in 515 BCE. The Second Temple stood until the Romans destroyed it in 70 CE, which forced many Jews to live in Diaspora (outside of Israel). Without the Temple, Jewish life changed. Worship focused on local synagogues and study of the Torah, led by rabbis. Important books like the Mishnah and Talmud were written to guide Jewish law and beliefs.

In the Middle Ages, Jews lived under Muslim and Christian rule. They often faced discrimination, but they also developed rich cultures such as Ashkenazi (Eastern Europe) and Sephardi (Spain and North Africa).

In modern times, Jews reacted to change in different ways. Some started Reform and Conservative movements, while others stayed Orthodox. The 1800s and 1900s brought new ideas like Zionism, which supported a Jewish homeland. After the Holocaust (when 6 million Jews were killed during World War II), the State of Israel was created in 1948. This became a major center of Jewish life.


Branches

Modern Judaism includes several main branches, each rooted in ancient tradition but differing in beliefs and practices. Orthodox Judaism believes the Torah (written and oral) is divine and unchanging, with strict observance of Jewish law. Within it, Haredi (ultra-Orthodox) Jews live in tight-knit communities, while Modern Orthodox Jews participate more in secular life while still following religious law.

Conservative Judaism arose as a middle ground. It values tradition but allows for change, seeing Jewish law as sacred but adaptable to modern life. This movement keeps many traditional practices while allowing innovations like female rabbis and mixed-gender worship.

Reform Judaism focuses on ethics and personal choice, seeing many traditional rituals as optional. Services are often in local languages, and gender roles are equal. It doesn’t see all Torah laws as binding but still emphasizes belief in God and social justice.

Smaller groups include Reconstructionist Judaism, which sees Judaism as a cultural civilization, and Renewal Judaism, which blends spirituality with progressive ideas. Many Jews identify as secular or cultural Jews. In the U.S., about 30–40% are Reform, 15–20% Conservative, around 10% Orthodox, with the rest spread across other or no branches. Despite differences, all branches share a connection to Jewish heritage.

Practices and Worships

Jewish religious life focuses on prayer, rituals, and community, with the Sabbath (Shabbat) as its centerpiece. From Friday evening to Saturday evening, Jews rest, attend synagogue, and enjoy special meals to honor God’s rest after creation. Shabbat begins with lighting candles and blessings over wine and bread, and it is observed by avoiding work and participating in prayer. Traditional daily worship includes three services, and on Shabbat or holidays, prayers are often done in a group. Important symbols include the tallit (prayer shawl) and tefillin (worn during weekday prayers in Orthodox communities). Torah readings are central, especially on the Sabbath, and some prayers require a minyan (ten adults).

The Jewish calendar includes many holidays. The most sacred are Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, a time for reflection and repentance. The three ancient pilgrimage festivals are Passover (remembering the Exodus), Shavuot (receiving the Torah), and Sukkot (celebrating the harvest in temporary shelters). Other holidays like Hanukkah and Purim are joyful and widely observed. Jewish life also includes life-cycle events such as circumcision, bar/bat mitzvahs, weddings, and funerals, all marked with specific customs. Kosher laws guide food choices, forbidding certain animals and mixing meat with dairy. While Orthodox Jews follow these laws strictly, Conservative Jews vary, and Reform Jews may observe them more flexibly. Together, these practices connect Jews to their faith and each other.

Society

Judaism is more than just a religion—it also has strong social and cultural roots. Community, education, and justice are important Jewish values. Traditions like tzedakah (charity) and tikkun olam (repairing the world) inspire Jews to help others and improve society. Jewish culture places high value on learning, both religious and secular. This focus on education has led many Jews to become leaders in fields like science, literature, and public service.

Jewish identity is also shaped by a strong sense of peoplehood. Even in places where Jews are a minority, they often build schools, charities, and community centers. Many Jews—especially in the U.S.—say they are proud of their heritage and feel deeply connected to other Jews and to Israel. Jewish life today can be religious, cultural, or both, but it usually includes a strong sense of history and shared values. Jews are active in both religious practices and community service, and they continue to fight against discrimination. Their traditions and experiences help shape modern, diverse societies.
