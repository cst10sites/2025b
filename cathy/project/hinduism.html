Hinduism

Introduction

Hinduism is one of the world’s oldest religions, originating in India without a single founder. It is based on ancient scriptures like the Vedas, Upanishads, Mahabharata (including the Bhagavad Gita), and Ramayana. Central beliefs include Brahman (universal soul), Atman (individual soul), samsara (cycle of rebirth), karma (cause and effect), dharma (duty), and moksha (liberation).

Core Beliefs

Hindus believe in a divine reality (Brahman) that exists within each soul (Atman). Life is cyclical, shaped by karma, and the goal is moksha—freedom from rebirth—achieved through ethical living, devotion, and self-realization. Deities like Vishnu, Shiva, and the Goddess (e.g., Durga, Lakshmi) are worshiped as aspects of Brahman.

Historical Background

Hinduism evolved from Vedic traditions over thousands of years. It absorbed elements from the ancient Indus Valley and later developed key ideas like karma and rebirth. Bhakti (devotional) movements flourished in the medieval period. Under Muslim and British rule, Hinduism changed, and reformers like Gandhi promoted values such as nonviolence (ahimsa). Today, Hinduism remains deeply tied to Indian identity and spreads globally.


Branches

  • Vaishnavism: Focus on Vishnu and his avatars like Krishna and Rama
  • Shaivism: Devotion to Shiva
  • Shaktism: Worship of the Goddess (Shakti)
  • Smartism: A philosophical tradition that honors five main deities equally, emphasizing unity

Practices and Worships

Worship (puja) includes offerings, chanting, and temple rituals. Many Hindus worship at home and in temples. Major festivals include Diwali, Holi, and Navaratri. Life-cycle rituals (samskaras) mark key stages of life. Pilgrimages to sacred sites like the Ganges or Kumbh Mela are important acts of devotion.

Society

Hinduism shapes South Asian society through values like dharma (duty), ahimsa (nonviolence), and seva (service). The caste system, though legally abolished, has influenced social structure. Hindu festivals are national holidays in India and Nepal, and Hindu communities worldwide preserve their traditions while adapting to local cultures. Hinduism encourages tolerance, charity, and respect for all living beings.
