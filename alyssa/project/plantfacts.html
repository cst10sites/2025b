Fireweed

Fireweed (Chamerion angustifolium) is a striking perennial herb that can grow up to 1.5 meters tall. It features vibrant magenta-pink flowers clustered along tall spikes and lance-shaped leaves. Fireweed thrives in disturbed or burned areas, often carpeted expansively after wildfires, helping stabilize soil and pioneer ecosystem recovery. It produces silky seeds that disperse easily on the wind. It’s widespread across temperate North America and Eurasia.


Indigenous connection

Indigenous peoples across the Pacific Northwest and beyond deeply valued fireweed for its multiple uses and symbolic meaning. The tender young shoots and leaves were harvested as a nutritious early-season green, either boiled or steamed. Fireweed tea was made from leaves and flowers for medicinal uses, treating digestive upset and respiratory issues. Its rapid regrowth after fires made it a potent symbol of renewal and hope in Indigenous stories and ceremonies, often teaching resilience and respect for nature’s cycles. Additionally, its soft seed hairs were used to stuff pillows or insulation, demonstrating ingenious use of available natural resources.

Nootka rose

Nootka rose (Rosa nutkana) is a robust, thorny shrub growing up to 3 meters tall with fragrant, five-petaled pink flowers that bloom in late spring to summer. It produces bright red, vitamin C-rich rose hips that persist into winter. The shrub favors forest edges, clearings, and riparian areas along the west coast of North America. The hips are edible, tart, and often harvested in autumn.


Indigenous connection

The Nootka rose was a vital food and medicine plant for many Indigenous peoples, including Coast Salish, Nuu-chah-nulth, and others. The hips were gathered in large quantities and eaten fresh, dried for winter sustenance, or cooked into jams and syrups, providing essential nutrients during lean months. Medicinally, rose hips and petals were used in teas and poultices for colds, wounds, and inflammation. The sturdy wood was used for basketry handles, arrow shafts, and other tools, reflecting sustainable harvesting practices and intimate ecological knowledge. Nootka rose often appeared in stories symbolizing strength and beauty intertwined with the natural world.

Pacific bleeding heart

Pacific bleeding heart (Dicentra formosa) is a delicate perennial wildflower growing in shady, moist forest understories from California to British Columbia. It features finely divided, fern-like foliage and arching stems with pendulous, heart-shaped flowers in shades of pink to purple, blooming in spring and early summer. It spreads through rhizomes to form colonies.


Indigenous connection

Although less commonly used as food, Pacific bleeding heart held importance in traditional medicine and spiritual practices. Indigenous healers applied plant extracts to ease headaches, minor skin irritations, and fevers. The plant’s presence was a sign of healthy forest ecosystems, which were central to Indigenous ways of life, encompassing hunting, gathering, and cultural ceremonies. Its fragile, heart-shaped flowers also inspired storytelling and symbolized love and connection in some Indigenous cultures.

Red columbine

Red columbine (Aquilegia formosa) is a showy perennial wildflower found in moist forests, meadows, and stream banks from Alaska to California. It has tubular red and yellow flowers that bloom from late spring to early summer and attract hummingbirds for pollination. The plant has finely divided leaves and grows to about 60 cm tall.


Indigenous connection

Indigenous peoples valued red columbine for its medicinal and symbolic properties. Roots and flowers were harvested for remedies addressing stomach aches, respiratory ailments, and sometimes as topical treatments for wounds. The vivid red flowers were often woven into cultural narratives symbolizing vitality, grace, and the interdependence of life forms, especially hummingbirds. Its bloom period often coincided with seasonal shifts, connecting plant cycles to Indigenous seasonal calendars.

Red huckleberry

Red huckleberry (Vaccinium parvifolium) is a deciduous shrub growing up to 3 meters tall in moist forests along the Pacific Northwest coast. It produces clusters of small, bright red berries in late summer that are sweet and juicy, beloved by wildlife and humans. The shrub has glossy, green leaves and thrives in shaded understories.


Indigenous connection

Red huckleberry was a key food source for Indigenous peoples, including the Coast Salish and other tribes. Berries were harvested by hand, eaten fresh, dried for winter stores, or cooked into preserves and pemmican. The berries were highly valued not only nutritionally but culturally, featuring in ceremonies and trade. Indigenous harvesters practiced stewardship by pruning and selectively harvesting to ensure sustainable yields. The wood was sometimes carved into tools, and the shrub’s presence indicated rich forest biodiversity essential for hunting and gathering.

Salmonberry

Salmonberry (Rubus spectabilis) is a fast-growing, deciduous shrub common in coastal and forested habitats along the Pacific Northwest. It produces soft, orange to red edible berries starting in early summer, often one of the first fruits to ripen. The large leaves are broad and often lobed, and the plant forms dense thickets.


Indigenous connection

Salmonberry was a highly valued seasonal food among Indigenous communities. The early ripening berries were eaten fresh, dried, or cooked into sauces and preserves, marking the beginning of the berry harvest season. Salmonberry thickets often grew near important fishing and hunting areas, linking food sources across seasons. The stems were used as natural fishing rods or weaving material for baskets and traps. Salmonberry’s prominence in the landscape also had symbolic meanings related to abundance and the renewal of life cycles.

Sword fern

Sword fern (Polystichum munitum) is a common, large evergreen fern found in the moist, shaded forests of the Pacific Northwest. It features long, arching, dark green fronds that can reach over 1.5 meters. Sword ferns grow in dense carpets on forest floors and are resilient in varied conditions.


Indigenous connection

Sword fern fronds played an important practical role in Indigenous life. They were used to line food storage baskets and boxes, keeping food fresh and preventing spoilage. The fronds also served as bedding material and insulation for carrying and storing items. Medicinally, sword fern was sometimes used as a poultice for wounds or to reduce swelling. It held significance as a marker of healthy forest ecosystems vital to Indigenous hunting and gathering practices, embodying a deep connection to forest stewardship.

Vine maple

Vine maple (Acer circinatum) is a small tree or large shrub native to moist, forested regions of the Pacific Northwest. It grows 3 to 10 meters tall with broad, five-lobed leaves that turn vivid orange, red, and yellow in fall. Vine maple thrives in understory environments and often forms dense thickets.


Indigenous connection

Indigenous peoples used vine maple wood extensively for making tools such as arrow shafts, digging sticks, and small implements due to its flexibility, strength, and fine grain. The colorful fall foliage was woven into stories and ceremonies that marked seasonal change and renewal. Vine maple branches and leaves also served as markers for territorial boundaries and resource areas. Its ecological role and cultural importance highlight the intertwined relationships Indigenous peoples maintain with forest plants.
